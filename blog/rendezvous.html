Music Video · Process

The Making of
RENDEZVOUS Music Video

Wonseop Kim Oct 22, 2024

RENDEZVOUS is a single written by KAMI, a musician at Vermilion Records. This orchestral pop song illustrates KAMI's complicated struggle with love through passionate vocal and performance. When I was brought on to direct the music video, I set three objectives for myself: deliver professional-quality content, properly manage and return all borrowed equipment, and build meaningful working relationships with the crew for future collaboration.

I have high standards for my work — when I say something could have been better, I'm not diminishing anyone's effort. I'm pointing out what I'd improve next time. That transparency is something I try to carry through every project.

Pre-Production

Assembling the crew

The production team grew to 18 filmmakers. I prioritised people I had worked with before, because technical proficiency and on-set chemistry are both essential — especially at this career stage. For departments I was less familiar with, like art direction and first AD, I shortlisted candidates and reviewed their work carefully before offering them a role.

Producer Yuki Ishigaki-Gilbert led logistics and kept everything on schedule. Having a strong producer is something I don't take for granted.

Developing the concept

My workflow always starts the same way: repeated listening. I put the song on loop and just let it sit with me before touching any document. Then I sat down with KAMI to develop the concept collaboratively — it's their story, and that conversation shapes everything.

From there, I built three foundational documents:

Audio-Visual Script — synchronising intended visuals with specific lyric moments.
Creative Brief — conveying the general theme through reference images and descriptive paragraphs.
Paper Edit / Shot List — conceptualising the entire music video including pacing and coverage before a traditional shot list was created.

The paper edit approach is something I use specifically for music video work. It lets me feel the rhythm of the edit before a single frame is shot, and it means the shot list that follows is purposeful rather than generic.

Locations & logistics

Three primary settings were identified — a house party, a performance stage, and a street location. We allocated two shooting days for the house party scenes and one day each for stage and street. We started a crowdfunding campaign to cover costs, but it didn't gain traction, so the team self-funded the production.

Equipment & technical planning

I worked closely with the cinematographer to finalise the equipment list. Our primary camera was a Panasonic Varicam LT. Additional lighting equipment came from Kanister Films at no cost, which was a significant help.

One of my favourite parts of pre-production on this shoot was the floor plan stage. The cinematographer, gaffer, and I sat down and drew out each location — identifying where lights could be safely rigged, where cameras could travel, what the sight lines were. It's a process I'd used before but rarely with this level of detail, and it paid off on the day.

Storyboard

The storyboard was the most difficult step in pre-production for me personally — I can't draw — but I worked closely with a visual artist to get every planned shot illustrated. Despite the difficulty, it remains the most effective way I know of communicating coverage to a crew before the day.

Production

On the day, my attention split equally between directing performance and directing the camera. We ran a two-camera setup, which turned out to be one of the most important decisions of the production.

The main reason for this production's success was the existence of a second camera — not restricted by the shot list, free to analyse the environment and shoot from unexpected angles.

I operated the primary camera according to the shot list, while the cinematographer had creative freedom on the secondary. That footage — spontaneous, reactive — is some of the strongest material in the final cut. It's something I'll build into every production going forward.

By the end of the shoot we had captured approximately 80 different shots across 284 total takes — all within the planned schedule.

Reflection on set leadership

The communication gaps I noticed on this project are something I've been honest with myself about. I used vague performance direction — words like "energetic" — where I should have given specific action direction. My feedback on shots was direct ("that's unusable") which works with experienced collaborators but can shut down people who are still building confidence.

I often comment on the quality of a shot by outright saying it's bad. For people who were trying to make the shot work, that can be very demotivating.

I'm not going to wholesale change my communication style — the directness produced a strong outcome here, and it's worked across multiple projects. But I do need to be able to switch modes depending on who I'm working with.

Post-Production

Editing

In the edit, I could see moments I'd missed during the shoot — specific instrumental hits, distinctive percussion moments where a perfectly timed cut would have landed. The final cut is still high quality, but I know what I'd go back and capture differently.

Editing sessions were collaborative — I worked closely with the editor, giving continuous feedback on cutting decisions rather than handing over a brief and stepping away. That back-and-forth produced a stronger result than either of us would have reached alone.

Colour grading

The theatre location used heavy purple theatrical lighting, which created significant complications in the grade — particularly balancing skin tones against darker facial hair areas under that colour temperature. We brought in an experienced filmmaker for external consultation and they identified a technical solution we hadn't considered. It's a reminder that knowing when to ask for help is a skill in itself.

The Final Video

KAMI — RENDEZVOUS (Official Music Video)

The completed video achieved the quality standard I set at the start of the project and opened up working relationships I've since built on. The three objectives I defined going in — quality, equipment responsibility, and crew relationships — were all met, even if there's a longer list of things I'd refine next time.

That longer list is the whole point. It's what makes the next project better.
